Materials for Conversation

The materials in action creating new ways of conversing in therapy.

Project was published in the International Journal of Design, 2024.

This project was a part of Audun Formo Hay's PhD, revolving around the theme of dignity in the Child Welfare System in B√¶rum. During the span of 7 weeks, I was to create and prototype a transformative design proposal focusing on dignity as a theme. Every week I met up with Caroline Alvestad, a family therapist, who was my collaborator during the project.

My proposal was a set of office materials that could be molded to fit different needs during the initial phase of therapy, when you are building an alliance with the patient. The material exploration consists of three components: The board, which is to symbolize the psychological phenomenon the "window of tolerance" and serve the purpose of a blank canvas. The "window of tolerance" is where you feel optimal, neither hypoaroused nor hyperaroused. The strings, as dynamic and temporary lines. And the whiteboard markers, with their erasable and proposing nature.

What the project aims to point out is that when working with the margins of society, there is a need for continuous adaptation rather than aiming for a one-size-fits-all model. By utilizing materials and their qualities, we can enable more dignified therapeutic conversations by letting more of the therapy be defined by the humans receiving it.

Facilitating for a creative exploratory work culture with gift baskets

Gift baskets for departments that are conservative and serious.

This one-week project worked with how to facilitate for workplace culture that cultivates creativity. We collaborated with "Oppdragslaboratoriet," a competency course for the Norwegian public administration where they teach design and innovation strategy. We looked into an issue they had with getting what they teach to be used in the workplaces that participants went back to after the course.

When interviewing the course responsible, we understood that in essence what they teach is techniques that are more exploratory. Upon closer examination, exploratory was just an administrative word for creativity. Building on this, we took a deep dive into research on creativity and made our own definition of creativity out of this.

The definition is made up of four elements: creativity as novel, as relevant, as deviation, or as safe. Now depending on what administrative department you went back to, you would need different tactics based on how that particular workplace culture is. We made up a matrix deciding what gift basket you were getting dependent on how serious to playful the department is, as well as how changeable to conservative.

Reflecting on the project, it has helped me understand the value of connecting knowledge and culture, and understanding the great potential that comes with balancing them.

A living strategy: Taking ownership of Oslo's streets

A part of building a new aesthetic for Levende gater, was to allow the citizens to take ownership of the street.

The final project of my BA was a collaboration with Oslo Municipality‚Äôs initiative called ‚ÄúLevende gater.‚Äù The initiative revolved around temporarily closing down traffic in certain streets to open up for neighborhood activities to stronger social fabric in the city. Me and Jenny worked with the interactions between the organizers and the other neighbors. Through our research, we understood the importance of three key values: inspiration, dialogue, and ownership. Our design proposal consists of three strategic tactics with an overall new aesthetic for the initiative.

In short, we created an idea bank for and by citizens to get inspired. For dialogue, we developed a physical invitation that was both low-threshold and personal. To build ownership, we identified loopholes where citizens could bend rules, such as tagging, but using chalk spray and markers instead. The project was very inspiring, as I have a profound interest in city planning and the everyday life of people in a society. Noticing nature

During guided meditation and embodiment.

As the final assignment for the course ‚ÄúCollaborative and Participatory Techniques in Design,‚Äù we were tasked to make a workshop of our choice utilizing what we had learned. In a group with three fellow students, we wanted to explore embodied knowledge in relation to the more-than-human.

As a vehicle for this, we designed a ritualistic journey consisting of a silent walk, prompted meditation, and ritualistic artifacts like pinecone necklaces. We got 5 participants to partake in the workshop, and throughout the experience, they (together with us) tried to embody being in the woods.

The result of the workshop was reflections on how the ritual made them feel about the nonhuman beings that surrounded them‚Äîgoing from a plant of moss to how moss as a body covers the whole forest floor, or how the reeds are not a plant but a motion in the wind of sound and force.

The project has taught me about how I relate to the forest, but also ways to design for reflection and connection with what we all too often take for granted.
